I work on autonomous systems. Every day, I watch them make thousands of micro-decisions — merging into traffic, judging gaps, adjusting speed — that they can't fully explain in human terms.

Passengers trust them. Regulators scrutinize them. And the question I keep coming back to isn't whether these systems are trustworthy. It's what, exactly, we're trusting them to do.

That question extends far beyond self-driving cars. Your credit card company trusts a model to flag fraud. Your bank trusts one to approve loans. A hiring tool screens resumes. These systems make consequential decisions constantly, and none of them can articulate their reasoning the way a human would.

The question isn't whether this is new. It's whether we're approaching it wisely.

## The verification gap

Here's the core problem: you can't ask an AI why it did something and get a reliable answer.

You can ask a human loan officer why they denied your application. You might disagree with their reasoning, but it's rooted in something — criteria, experience, judgment they can defend.

An AI's explanation is often a post-hoc reconstruction, not the actual decision process. The system that made the call and the system that explains the call are sometimes different things entirely.

I think of this as the **verification gap**: the distance between what a system does and what you can confirm about how and why it does it.

With human decisions, the gap is small. With AI, it's enormous. Close the gap and trust becomes reasonable. Widen it and trust becomes a bet.

Organizations that take this seriously close the gap through obsessive testing, simulation, and failure analysis. But most companies deploying AI don't have that discipline — and the stakes still matter.

## Why "working most of the time" is dangerous

Antilock brakes pulse hydraulics faster than any human could react. You don't understand the mechanism. You trust them because they fail predictably — you know what happens, you've tested it, it's consistent.

AI doesn't fail predictably. It works 98% of the time, then suddenly does something inexplicable. A fraud system that blocks 99% of actual fraud sounds great. But what about one that occasionally blocks all transactions from a specific region by accident?

Same aggregate performance. Wildly different failure properties.

Working in autonomy taught me this: the aggregate success rate is less important than the shape of the failure distribution. A system that fails in predictable, bounded ways is fundamentally more trustworthy than one that fails rarely but catastrophically.

## What you can actually evaluate

If you can't see inside the black box, focus on these:

**How does it fail under stress?** Test with weird inputs — contradictions, ambiguity, edge cases. A system that escalates gracefully when uncertain is more trustworthy than one that confidently guesses.

**How predictable are the failure modes?** "Our model struggles with unstructured input" is fixable. "It sometimes fails mysteriously" is dangerous. Understanding failure boundaries lets you design around them.

**Does it know what it doesn't know?** A model that outputs 95% confidence when it should be at 40% is actively misleading you. Calibrated uncertainty is one of the most important — and most undervalued — properties in any deployed system.

**Who's accountable when it breaks?** A system backed by clear ownership, insurance, and audit processes is more trustworthy than one where responsibility dissolves into "the algorithm decided." This isn't a technical question, but it matters as much as the technical ones.

## Mapping trust instead of giving verdicts

Here's the framework I've landed on: stop asking "is this trustworthy?" and start asking "what, specifically, am I trusting this to do?"

Trust isn't binary. It's contextual.

You trust Google Maps because it fails gracefully (worst case, a longer route), you can verify easily (you'll notice if you're lost), the consequences are mild, and it's been tested by millions.

You should not blindly trust an untested hiring AI because it fails opaquely, you can't easily verify, the consequences are high (someone's career), and it's only been validated on your data.

Same confidence scores. Wildly different trust profiles.

The map you need is: in which contexts, for which decisions, with which consequences, is this system worth trusting?

## Practical takeaways

If you're deploying AI:

Start with low stakes — use it for recommendations, not final decisions. Let humans override easily. Build observability — log what the system decided and the confidence behind it. You'll need that audit trail. Test edge cases deliberately — not just on training data, but on contradictions, ambiguity, and adversarial inputs. Plan for failure — not if, but when. Who overrides? How do humans know they should intervene? What's the escalation path?

If you're subject to an AI system:

Ask who specifically stands behind it. Ask what kinds of decisions it gets wrong. Request a human override path. Pay attention to calibration — if the system is confident about genuinely uncertain things, that's a red flag.

## The vertigo is the feature

I won't pretend we've solved this. We haven't.

The most capable systems are often the least interpretable. The safer systems are often less capable. There's a fundamental tradeoff, and we're still learning to navigate it.

But the discomfort becomes manageable when you stop trying to deliver a verdict and start building a map — specific situations, specific safeguards, specific decisions where trust is reasonable.

That map is harder to build than a binary yes or no. But it's the only approach that scales.
