For a while, the default AI question was: *Which model is best?*

Increasingly, I think the more strategic question is: *Which ecosystem is easiest for other agents, tools, and businesses to plug into?*

Models create moments. Protocols create markets.

## The interoperability thesis

In the next phase of AI, winning won't just mean being smartest. It will mean being easiest to discover, authenticate, trust, and transact with.

That changes where value accumulates. Less in closed demos. More in the standards that let systems work together.

MCP, A2A, UCP, and official machine-readable documentation layers are becoming real pieces of the stack — not just developer experiments. They represent something more consequential than technical plumbing: they're the early infrastructure of an economy where software agents participate in workflows alongside humans.

We've seen this in earlier platform cycles. The winners weren't always the flashiest products. They were the layers everyone else had to integrate with. TCP/IP. HTTP. OAuth. REST. GraphQL. Each of these became powerful not because they were the most elegant solution, but because they reduced the cost of connection below the threshold where connection became default behavior.

## Why this cycle is different

The AI version of this pattern has a twist. In previous platform cycles, the things connecting to each other were mostly passive — databases, APIs, web pages. They waited to be called.

In the agent era, the things connecting to each other have *intent*. They initiate. They decide. They route. They negotiate.

That means interoperability stops being a technical nice-to-have. It becomes distribution.

If agents become real participants in workflows, the question isn't just "can they talk to each other?" It's "can they trust each other?" Can they verify identity? Can they negotiate terms? Can they handle failure gracefully when the other side does something unexpected?

## The design implication

The companies that understand this early will design for inclusion in the agent ecosystem. They'll invest in:

- Machine-readable product documentation
- Stable, well-documented APIs with predictable behavior
- Authentication patterns that work for non-human clients
- Structured data that agents can reason about without scraping

The ones that don't may discover that in the AI era, being excellent but hard to connect to is a form of invisibility.

This is the same lesson the mobile era taught. The apps that won weren't always the best-designed. They were the ones in the app store, with deep links, with share extensions, with integrations that made them show up in other people's workflows.

## In the next platform cycle

Interface matters. Protocol decides scale.

The companies that are building the best models will get attention. The companies that are building the best protocols will get adoption. And in platform economics, adoption is what compounds.
