For the last two years, most AI conversations have focused on output.

Faster writing. Faster research. Faster analysis.

That's the first-order effect.

The second-order effect is more interesting: once intelligence gets cheaper, coordination gets more expensive.

Not because people matter less. Because unmanaged abundance creates noise.

When everyone can generate strategy decks, market maps, forecasts, and recommendations on demand, advantage shifts to a different layer:

**Who frames the question well.** The quality of the answer is now bounded by the quality of the prompt — and the quality of the prompt is bounded by clarity of strategic intent. Models don't fix ambiguous mandates. They amplify them.

**Who decides faster.** When analysis is instant, the bottleneck moves from "we don't have the data" to "we don't have agreement on what to do with it." Decision latency becomes more visible and more costly.

**Who resolves exceptions cleanly.** AI handles the predictable middle of most distributions well. But the edges — the weird cases, the novel situations, the things that require judgment — still need humans. And how fast those exceptions get resolved now determines throughput.

**Who redesigns workflows instead of just accelerating them.** The least valuable use of AI is to make a broken process faster. The most valuable use is to notice that the process should not exist at all.

## This is a management story

That's why I think AI is less a software story than a management story.

The winners won't just be the companies with the best models. They'll be the ones with the cleanest decision rights, the best escalation paths, and the discipline to separate signal from synthetic volume.

Most organizations are not bottlenecked by intelligence. They are bottlenecked by:

- Unclear ownership of decisions
- Too many review layers for low-stakes changes
- Incentive structures that reward busyness over clarity
- No shared framework for what "good enough" looks like

AI doesn't fix any of that. It makes all of it more visible.

## What to watch for

When I look at how organizations are deploying AI, I pay less attention to which tools they bought and more attention to whether they've changed how decisions flow.

The first wave was: "Let's give everyone access to a model."

The second wave — the one that matters — is: "Let's redesign who decides what, now that information is abundant."

AI doesn't just compress the cost of thinking. It raises the premium on organizational clarity.

When intelligence becomes abundant, operating discipline compounds.
