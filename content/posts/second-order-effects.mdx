Every time a major technology shifts, the same pattern plays out. Everyone focuses on the obvious, first-order effect. Fewer people notice the second-order consequences. And almost nobody anticipates the third.

Cars didn't just replace horses. They created suburbs, changed dating culture, built an oil economy, spawned fast food, and reshaped American politics. Nobody in 1910 was talking about drive-throughs.

AI is in its "horseless carriage" moment. The conversation is dominated by first-order thinking: which jobs will AI replace? How much faster will it make knowledge work? Will it write better code than humans?

These are fine questions. But they're not the interesting questions. The interesting questions are about what happens next — the cascading consequences that only become visible when you follow the chain.

## The chain reaction problem

Most people think linearly. A causes B. AI automates X job, therefore X workers need new jobs. That's first-order thinking and it's useful for short-term planning.

But systems don't work linearly. They cascade. A causes B, which causes C and D, which interact to produce E — which nobody predicted because nobody was looking at C and D at the same time.

I spend a lot of time thinking about this at Waymo. Self-driving cars aren't just a transportation technology. They're a cascade machine. And the downstream effects are going to be stranger and more consequential than most people expect.

## The insurance question

Let me take one chain and go deeper. If autonomous vehicles reduce accidents by 90% — which is a plausible long-term scenario based on current safety data — what happens to auto insurance?

The obvious answer: it shrinks. Fewer accidents, fewer claims, lower premiums, less revenue.

But that's only the first domino. Auto insurance is a $300 billion industry in the US alone. Insurance companies are major investors in real estate, bonds, and equities. Their investment income often exceeds their underwriting income. If the auto insurance market shrinks by half, you're pulling hundreds of billions out of capital markets.

That's a second-order effect that has nothing to do with driving.

Go one more step. Insurance companies employ hundreds of thousands of people — not just adjusters and underwriters, but actuaries, data scientists, marketing teams, customer service reps, compliance officers. These are middle-class knowledge worker jobs in every city in the country.

And the cascade continues. Those former insurance employees need new work. Local real estate markets that depend on insurance company office space take a hit. The political power of the insurance lobby — which has shaped regulation in every state for decades — diminishes. New regulatory frameworks emerge for vehicle manufacturer liability. Product liability lawyers become more important than personal injury lawyers.

None of this is about AI directly. It's all downstream.

## Why second-order thinking matters for strategy

If you're a business leader making decisions about AI, first-order thinking is necessary but insufficient.

First-order thinking says: "We should adopt AI to reduce costs in our claims processing department."

Second-order thinking says: "If everyone adopts AI for claims processing, what happens to the labor market for claims adjusters? How does that affect our recruitment pipeline for related roles? If claims processing becomes commoditized, where does competitive advantage shift? Probably to fraud detection and customer experience — should we be investing there instead?"

The teams that think at this level are the ones that make the right bets. Not because they can predict the future — nobody can. But because they're asking better questions about which futures are plausible and positioning for multiple scenarios.

## The patterns to watch

After spending years thinking about cascading effects, I've noticed a few patterns that show up repeatedly.

**When AI eliminates a bottleneck, the system speeds up — and the next bottleneck gets exposed.** AI makes generating content easy. Suddenly distribution is the bottleneck. AI makes diagnosis fast. Suddenly treatment capacity is the bottleneck. Watch for the thing that was never the limiting factor suddenly becoming one.

**When AI reduces the cost of something, demand for its complements increases.** AI makes data analysis cheap. Demand for people who can ask good questions about data goes up. AI makes code generation fast. Demand for people who can architect systems goes up. The complement, not the substitute, is where the value migrates.

**When AI makes something abundant, scarcity shifts to trust.** AI can generate a million articles. The scarce resource isn't content — it's knowing which content to trust. AI can produce legal analysis instantly. The scarce resource isn't the analysis — it's knowing whether to rely on it. Every abundance creates a new scarcity, and the new scarcity is almost always about trust, judgment, or curation.

## The question nobody's asking

Here's what I keep coming back to: the biggest changes from AI won't be the ones we're debating right now. They'll be the ones that seem unrelated to AI entirely.

Some city planner in 2035 will deal with a parking crisis — not too few spaces, but too many, because half the cars on the road are autonomous and they don't park downtown anymore. Some insurance executive will have to explain why their century-old business model doesn't work in a world with 90% fewer accidents. Some university president will face enrollment decline not because their education is bad, but because an AI tutor made it irrelevant for half their students.

These problems will feel surprising when they arrive. But they're not unpredictable. They're just two steps downstream from changes that are already underway.

The people who will navigate the AI transition best aren't the ones building the models. They're the ones thinking about what the models change — two steps out, three steps out, into territory that feels speculative now but will feel obvious in retrospect.

Start following the chains. The interesting stuff is always one more step out than you think.
