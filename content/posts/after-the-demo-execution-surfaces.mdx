Watch an employee do their job for a day. Book a meeting with a customer. Prep materials for the call. Send a follow-up after.

Six steps. Four different apps. Calendar. Email. Docs. Slack. Click, switch, click, switch, copy-paste context, wait for things to load, lose your train of thought.

Tomorrow that collapses into one sentence: "Book a call with this customer, prep materials for Q3 planning, send me a draft follow-up when done."

One surface. One intent. Everything else happens behind the scenes.

That's the move nobody's talking about because they're too busy arguing about which model is smarter.

## The unbundling era is backwards now

For fifteen years software went one direction: specialization. Email spun out. Then docs. Then messaging. Then project management. Then calendar. You ended up with ten, twelve, fifteen tools. Each one was best-in-class at its one thing. That was the strategy. Unbundled. Modular. Pure.

AI is flipping that. Not by building bloated super-apps that try to do everything. That doesn't work. But by building a layer above everything that coordinates across all of it.

You express intent to that layer. It pulls from your calendar. Writes in your docs. Posts in Slack. Executes the workflow. Reports back when it's done.

You never touch the individual apps.

That top layer becomes the center of gravity. Whoever owns it owns the user. The apps below it become suppliers.

## Why this matters

Slack knows this. Google knows this. Apple knows this. They're all spending money to be that orchestration layer.

But here's the thing: the winner probably won't be a new company. It'll be whatever product you use most — the thing closest to daily work. For most knowledge workers, that's a chat interface or email. For sales, the CRM. For engineers, the code editor.

Whichever surface you touch most, multiple times a day. That surface wins because it becomes the natural place to express intent.

Not the tool with a thousand features. The tool you already live in.

## What this destroys

Feature depth dies here.

"Feature-rich" doesn't matter anymore. Asana can have a thousand features, Linear can have five hundred. If the orchestration layer routes your task to whichever tool makes sense, the feature count is irrelevant.

Lock-in evaporates. Switching email clients is painful today because years of history live there. But if an orchestration layer pulls context from any email system, that lock-in disappears.

"Best of breed" adoption doesn't work the same way. Companies used to say: Jira for this team, Linear for that team, GitHub Projects for engineering. The orchestration layer makes those decisions invisible. It routes the work to whatever tool fits.

The competitive game moved up. Your tool's features matter less than whether you integrate cleanly with the layer above you.

## What actually matters now

If you build tools, here's what you should care about:

**Tight integrations.** Not APIs where developers build custom stuff. Seamless connections that an AI system can trust, query, and execute actions in. Predictably.

**Context you can add.** When work gets routed to your system, can you hand the orchestration layer something useful? User history. What's likely next. Relevant info that helps the AI make good decisions.

**Fail gracefully.** AI hits an edge case in your system? Don't break the workflow. Escalate it clearly. Surface it for humans to decide.

**Zero setup.** If your tool needs signup, configuration, onboarding, the orchestration layer won't route there. It'll go somewhere that works immediately.

## The real opportunity

Every team does work across five, ten, sometimes fifteen tools. Most people try to fix this by replacing one tool with a better tool. That misses it.

The smarter move is building the orchestration layer for one specific type of work.

Hiring orchestration. Connect your ATS, email, calendar, Slack, offer letters. Let an AI handle the workflow end-to-end. You're not replacing any of those tools. You're wrapping them.

Sales orchestration. CRM, email, calendar, documents, contracts. Route actions intelligently. Escalate when it gets complicated.

Whoever owns the orchestration layer for a specific workflow owns that workflow. Even if they don't own the individual pieces.

## What to measure

Track these:

**Time from intent to done.** Should be measured in seconds, not hours.

**Context switching.** How many apps to complete one workflow? You want this trending toward zero.

**Routing accuracy.** How often does the system send work to the right tool without bouncing it back?

**Handoff friction.** When humans need to jump in, how fast does it resolve?

These matter more than feature count or monthly actives.

The old game was search engines fighting. Chrome fighting Firefox. Slack fighting Teams. Surface against surface.

The new game is simpler: who owns the place where intent gets expressed? Whoever answers that question owns the workflow, the traffic, and the relationship.
