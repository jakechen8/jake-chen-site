We still talk about search as though it exists to help people find information.

That definition is getting old.

The new surface is not search. It's delegated intent.

## Beyond the answer

The product that wins won't just answer questions. It will remember context, narrow options, coordinate tools, and move a task forward.

That is why search, browsers, and productivity software are starting to blur together. OpenAI and Google are both moving from answer interfaces toward surfaces that remember context, build artifacts, and complete tasks.

One layer captures intent. Another brings context. Another takes action.

Eventually, users won't care which category a product used to belong to. They'll care whether it moves them from:

*"I need to do this"*

to

*"It's done."*

## The unbundling is actually a rebundling

We spent a decade unbundling. Email was separate from docs was separate from project management was separate from search. Each product had a lane.

AI is rebundling — not by creating one super-app, but by creating a layer that sits above all of them and orchestrates on the user's behalf.

The interesting question isn't which app survives. It's which *surface* becomes the entry point. Because whoever owns that layer gets privileged access to routing, recommendations, and next actions.

This is the classic platform move: own the point of intent, and everything downstream becomes a supplier.

## What this means for strategy

The battle isn't just for attention anymore. It's for the right to orchestrate what happens after attention.

That has big strategic consequences:

**For productivity tools:** The moat is no longer features. It's context. If your product knows what the user did yesterday and what they're likely to need tomorrow, you have an advantage that no feature checklist can replicate.

**For search engines:** The business model needs to evolve from "send users elsewhere" to "complete the task here." That's a different product, a different revenue model, and a different competitive position.

**For browsers:** The browser was always the most undervalued surface — it sees everything the user does. AI makes that visibility strategic.

**For startups:** There's an opening for products that don't compete in any traditional category but instead compete for the first expression of user intent. The "I need to..." moment. If you can capture and route that moment, the rest is execution.

## The first mile of action

The next platform power may belong less to the app where work is completed and more to the surface where intent is first expressed.

The next interface war is really about who owns the first mile of action.

And the winners will be the products that understand: in a world of abundant intelligence, the most valuable thing isn't the answer. It's the ability to act on it without switching contexts.
