Your employee needs to book a meeting with a customer, prepare for it, and send a follow-up.

Today that's six steps across four apps — calendar, email, docs, Slack. Tomorrow it's one sentence: "Book a call with this customer, prep materials for Q3 planning, send me a draft follow-up when done."

The difference isn't that AI is faster. It's that a single surface handles the entire workflow, not just one piece of it.

That's the competitive battle most companies are missing.

## The unbundling era is reversing

For fifteen years, software went one direction: unbundling. Email was its own company. Docs were separate. Messaging was separate. Project management was separate. Calendar was separate. You were using eight to twelve tools to get work done.

AI is reversing that — not by creating bloated super-apps, but by creating a new layer above all those tools that coordinates across them.

You tell the top layer what you want. It pulls context from your calendar, writes in your docs, posts in your Slack, executes the workflow across all of them, and reports back when done.

You never open the individual apps.

That top layer is the new center of gravity. Whoever owns it owns the relationship with the user. Everything else becomes a supplier to it.

This is something I think about a lot from a startup perspective. If you're building a new company today, the question isn't just "what does your product do?" It's "are you the orchestration layer or are you a component that someone else's layer will coordinate?"

## Why this matters for competitive strategy

Slack, Google, Apple — they all see this. Slack is investing in workflow orchestration. Gmail is becoming a task surface and scheduling surface. Siri is trying to be the orchestration layer for Apple's ecosystem.

But the winner probably won't be a new company. It will be the product closest to daily workflow that can credibly orchestrate everything else. For knowledge workers, that's likely a chat interface, email client, or productivity workspace. For sales teams, it's the CRM. For engineering teams, the code editor.

The top layer wins. Not the component with the best feature set.

## What this kills

This shift systematically eliminates competitive advantages in feature depth.

It kills "feature-rich" as a moat. It doesn't matter that Asana has a thousand features and Linear has five hundred if the orchestration layer routes your request to whichever one serves the use case.

It kills switching costs. Today, changing email clients is painful because your history lives there. But if an orchestration layer pulls context from any email system, the lock-in evaporates.

It kills "best of breed" adoption. Companies used to pick specialists for each team — Jira here, Linear there, GitHub Projects over there. Orchestration layers route different requests to different tools transparently.

The competitive arena moved upward. The individual tool's features matter less than whether it integrates cleanly with the orchestration layer.

## What this creates demand for

If you build productivity tools or platforms, here's what matters now:

**Tight integrations.** Not APIs that developers build on, but seamless connections that orchestration layers leverage. An AI system needs to trust your tool, query it, and execute actions in it predictably.

**Context awareness.** Can you enrich the orchestration layer with context? When a task gets routed to your system, can you provide user history, likely next steps, and relevant information?

**Graceful failure.** When an AI-orchestrated workflow hits an edge case in your system, surface it clearly enough for human intervention. Don't break the workflow — escalate it.

**Zero friction.** The orchestration layer is impatient. If your system needs signup, setup, configuration, or training, it won't get routed there. Plug-and-play is the price of admission.

## The startup opportunity

There's a significant opportunity here, and it's one I keep coming back to.

Every team has jobs they do across five to ten different tools. Most startups try to replace one tool with a better tool. The smarter move is to build the orchestration layer for a specific type of work.

Hiring team orchestration — connect your ATS, email, calendar, Slack, and offer letter tool. Let AI handle the workflow. You're not replacing any of those tools. You're wrapping all of them.

Sales workflow orchestration — connect CRM, email, calendar, document storage, and contracts. Route actions intelligently. Escalate when it gets complicated.

The companies that own the orchestration layer for a specific workflow own that workflow, even without owning the individual components. That's the kind of business I find most compelling to think about building — and it's the lens through which I evaluate a lot of what's happening in AI right now.

## What to measure

If you're a product leader, track time between intent and completion (should be seconds, not hours), context switching (how many apps to complete one workflow — trending toward zero), orchestration routing (how often the system routes to the right tool without escalation), and handoff friction (when human judgment is needed, how quickly does escalation resolve).

These matter more than feature count or active users.

The old battles were Google vs. Yahoo, Chrome vs. Firefox, Slack vs. Teams. The new battle is: who owns the surface where intent is expressed? Whoever controls that gets to define the workflow, route the traffic, and capture the relationship.
